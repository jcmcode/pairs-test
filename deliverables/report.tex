% ABSTRACT
% ============================================================================
\begin{abstract}
We present a clustering-based framework for discovering tradeable pairs among 40 semiconductor stocks using 11 months of hourly data. Three unsupervised learning algorithms---OPTICS, DBSCAN, and KMeans---are applied independently to rolling feature snapshots to identify stocks exhibiting similar short-term behaviour. Despite fundamental differences in their clustering mechanics, all three algorithms converge on the same top pairs, revealing genuine subsector relationships (equipment, RF, analog, EDA) without any fundamental data as input. We validate discovered pairs using classical pairs trading criteria---Engle-Granger cointegration, half-life of mean reversion, and the Hurst exponent---with a strict 67/33 calibration/out-of-sample split. Of 33 filtered pairs, 26 pass at least two of three criteria, and 14 of 26 tradeable pairs produce positive out-of-sample P\&L under a z-score mean-reversion strategy. We document both the strengths and limitations of this approach, including a detailed analysis of look-ahead bias and the finding that cross-algorithm consensus does not predict out-of-sample profitability.

\section{Introduction}

% TODO: Expand with broader motivation---why pairs trading, why semiconductors,
% why clustering as an alternative to traditional cointegration screening.

Pairs trading is among the oldest systematic strategies in quantitative finance, yet the first step---\textit{which} pairs to trade---is often approached naively. Traditional methods screen for cointegration across all $\binom{N}{2}$ combinations, which scales poorly and produces many spurious results.

We propose an alternative: use unsupervised clustering on real-time feature data to let the algorithm \textit{discover} which stocks are behaving similarly at any given moment. When two stocks consistently land in the same cluster, it signals a relationship worth investigating with classical statistical tests.

This report applies three clustering algorithms to 40 semiconductor stocks over approximately 11 months of hourly data (March 2025 to February 2026). The key questions are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Do clustering algorithms reliably identify structurally related pairs?
    \item Do the discovered pairs exhibit mean-reverting spreads suitable for pairs trading?
    \item How do the three algorithms compare, and does cross-algorithm agreement add value?
    \item Do results survive honest out-of-sample validation?
\end{enumerate}

\section{Data and Feature Engineering}

\subsection{Universe and Data}

Our universe consists of 40 semiconductor tickers spanning megacap leaders (NVDA, TSM, AVGO), equipment manufacturers (AMAT, LRCX, KLAC), specialized chip companies (QRVO, SWKS, NXPI), EDA vendors (CDNS, SNPS), and mid-caps (GFS, MRVL, CAMT). Hourly OHLCV data is sourced from Yahoo Finance, covering March 11, 2025 to February 9, 2026 ($\sim$1,580 hourly timestamps).

The regime shift features (short-window minus medium-window) are designed to detect \textit{when} a stock's behaviour is changing relative to its recent baseline---exactly the signal relevant for transient relationship formation.

Before clustering, features are standardized (zero mean, unit variance) and reduced via PCA retaining 90\% of explained variance.

% 3. CLUSTERING METHODOLOGY
% ============================================================================
\section{Clustering Methodology}

We apply three fundamentally different clustering algorithms to the same feature data, running each independently at every valid hourly timestamp.

\subsection{OPTICS}

OPTICS (Ordering Points To Identify the Clustering Structure) is a density-based algorithm that does not require specifying the number of clusters \textit{a priori}. It identifies clusters of varying density and assigns a noise label ($-1$) to points that do not fit any cluster.

\textbf{Parameters:} \texttt{min\_samples=3}, \texttt{xi=0.05}, \texttt{min\_cluster\_size=3}.

OPTICS produces a high noise rate ($\sim$58\%), meaning most tickers at most timestamps are not assigned to any cluster. This is by design---it only forms clusters when there is genuine feature similarity. We address the resulting frequency bias through noise-adjusted co-clustering rates (Section~\ref{sec:noise}).

\subsection{DBSCAN}

DBSCAN uses a fixed density threshold ($\varepsilon$) and requires a minimum number of points to form a cluster. We use an adaptive $\varepsilon$ selection based on the k-nearest-neighbour distance distribution at each timestamp.

\textbf{Noise rate:} $\sim$28\%. Lower than OPTICS because the fixed threshold is less conservative.

\subsection{KMeans}

KMeans is centroid-based and assigns every point to a cluster (no native noise concept). We select $k$ at each timestamp via silhouette score optimization and apply a post-hoc noise filter: tickers with silhouette score below a threshold are reclassified as noise.

\textbf{Effective noise rate:} $\sim$10\% after filtering.

\subsection{Noise-Adjusted Co-Clustering Frequency}
\label{sec:noise}

For density-based algorithms, raw co-clustering frequency (count / total timestamps) is misleading because many timestamps have one or both tickers in noise. We define:

\[
f_{\text{adj}}(A, B) = \frac{\text{co-cluster count}(A, B)}{|\{t : A \notin \text{noise at } t \text{ and } B \notin \text{noise at } t\}|}
\]

This measures: \textit{when both stocks are actually in real clusters, how often are they in the same one?}

\subsection{Statistical Significance}

We validate co-clustering significance using a feature-shuffle permutation test. At each of 80 sampled timestamps across 30 permutations, we shuffle feature vectors across tickers (preserving cross-feature correlation but breaking the ticker-feature mapping) and re-run the full StandardScaler $\to$ PCA $\to$ OPTICS pipeline.

Eleven pairs achieve $Z > 1.96$ (significant at $p < 0.05$), confirming their co-clustering frequency exceeds what would arise from random feature assignment.

\section{Pair Validation}

\subsection{Classical Pairs Trading Criteria}

We test each discovered pair (noise-adjusted frequency $> 0.15$, yielding 33 pairs) against three classical pairs trading criteria, computed on the \textbf{calibration window only} (first 67\% of daily observations):

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Engle-Granger cointegration} ($p < 0.05$, tested in both directions, minimum $p$-value used)
    \item \textbf{Half-life of mean reversion} (5--60 days, estimated via AR(1) regression on the spread)
    \item \textbf{Hurst exponent} ($< 0.5$, estimated via variance ratio method)
\end{enumerate}

Hedge ratios are estimated via OLS on the calibration window. The spread is defined as $S_t = P_t^A - \hat{\beta} P_t^B$.

\subsection{Results}

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Pair validation summary (calibration window).}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lcl@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Category} & \textbf{Count} & \textbf{Description} \\
\midrule
Tested          & 33 & Noise-adjusted frequency $> 0.15$ \\
Passed (3/3)    & 4  & Cointegrated + half-life 5--60d + Hurst $< 0.5$ \\
Near-miss (2/3) & 22 & Typically passed half-life + Hurst, failed cointegration \\
Failed (0--1/3) & 7  & Trending spread or impractical half-life \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\label{tab:validation-summary}
\end{table}

The four pairs passing all three criteria are ADI--NXPI, ADI--QRVO, ADI--SWKS, and QCOM--QRVO. The 22 near-miss pairs typically exhibit mean-reverting behaviour (Hurst $< 0.5$, reasonable half-life) but fail formal cointegration. This is consistent with the transient nature of the relationships: they form and dissolve over time, so a test assuming permanent equilibrium often rejects.

The 7 failures include equipment pairs (AMAT--LRCX, AMAT--KLAC) where the spread \textit{trends} rather than reverts (Hurst $> 0.5$), and STX--WDC where the half-life (96.6 days) is too slow for practical trading.

\section{Out-of-Sample Backtesting}

\subsection{Strategy}

All 26 tradeable pairs (passed or near-miss) are backtested on the \textbf{out-of-sample window} (last 33\% of daily data, $\sim$3.5 months) using a standard z-score mean-reversion strategy:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Lookback:} 20 days rolling mean and standard deviation
    \item \textbf{Entry:} $|z| \geq 2.0$ (long spread when $z \leq -2$, short when $z \geq 2$)
    \item \textbf{Exit:} $|z| \leq 0.5$
    \item \textbf{Hedge ratio:} Fixed, estimated on calibration window via OLS
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Important caveat:} Trade counts are low (2--5 per pair over $\sim$3.5 months). While the directional results are encouraging, they are not statistically sufficient to draw strong conclusions about individual pair profitability.

\subsection{Look-Ahead Bias Analysis}

An earlier version of this analysis estimated hedge ratios on the full data period and backtested on the same data, yielding 23/23 pairs profitable with 80--100\% win rates. After implementing the proper calibration/OOS split:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Profitable pairs dropped from 23/23 to 14/26
    \item Several pairs that looked excellent in-sample showed OOS losses (e.g., ADI--NXPI: in-sample Sharpe 1.08 $\to$ OOS Sharpe $-1.76$)
    \item Top performers remained strong, confirming genuine signal
\end{itemize}

This underscores the critical importance of out-of-sample validation in any quantitative strategy.

This is an important negative result: agreement among algorithms validates the relationship but does not substitute for proper out-of-sample testing of the trading strategy.

\section{Discovered Subsector Structure}

A notable byproduct of this analysis is that the clustering algorithms---which receive only price-derived features, no fundamental data---independently discover the semiconductor industry's subsector structure:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Equipment cluster:} AMAT, LRCX, KLAC --- shared customers (TSMC, Samsung, Intel fabs), driven by semiconductor capex cycles
    \item \textbf{RF cluster:} QRVO, SWKS, QCOM --- tied to smartphone and 5G demand
    \item \textbf{Analog cluster:} ADI, NXPI, TXN, MCHP --- shared industrial and automotive end markets
    \item \textbf{EDA cluster:} CDNS, SNPS --- the chip design software duopoly
\end{itemize}

This convergence between data-driven clustering and known industry structure provides additional confidence that the discovered pairs reflect genuine economic relationships.

\section{Discussion}

% TODO: Expand with your own interpretation and takeaways.

\subsection{Strengths}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Methodological rigour:} Proper calibration/OOS split, bidirectional cointegration testing, feature-shuffle permutation tests, and noise-adjusted frequencies eliminate common sources of bias.
    \item \textbf{Multi-algorithm validation:} Testing three fundamentally different algorithms on the same data provides cross-validation. The convergence on the same top pairs is compelling evidence that these relationships are real.
    \item \textbf{Intellectually honest:} We report that only $\sim$54\% of tradeable pairs are profitable OOS. The look-ahead bias lesson is documented explicitly.
    \item \textbf{Industry structure discovery:} The algorithms uncover known subsector groupings from raw price data alone, validating the feature engineering approach.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Low trade counts:} 2--5 OOS trades per pair over $\sim$3.5 months is insufficient for statistical confidence in individual pair results.
    \item \textbf{Single strategy tested:} Only a simple z-score mean-reversion strategy with fixed parameters was backtested. Adaptive hedge ratios (e.g., Kalman filter), alternative entry/exit rules, or regime-aware position sizing could improve results.
    \item \textbf{Fixed hedge ratios:} OLS hedge ratios estimated on the calibration window are static. Several pairs that lost money OOS may have suffered from hedge ratio drift.
    \item \textbf{Sector concentration:} All 40 stocks are semiconductors. The approach should be tested on a broader or different universe.
    \item \textbf{Transaction costs:} Backtests assume zero trading costs.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Future Work}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Walk-forward validation:} Replace the single 67/33 split with rolling walk-forward windows for more robust performance estimates.
    \item \textbf{Adaptive hedge ratios:} Use Kalman filter-estimated hedge ratios that evolve with the data, rather than fixed OLS estimates.
    \item \textbf{Real-time pipeline:} The codebase includes prototype functions for real-time formation detection and signal generation. Wiring these into a live pipeline would enable the original transient trading thesis.
    \item \textbf{Expanded universe:} Apply the framework to other sectors or cross-sector to test generalizability.
    \item \textbf{Transaction cost modelling:} Incorporate realistic spread, slippage, and commission assumptions.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}

% TODO: Write your own conclusion. Below is a starting point.

We demonstrate that unsupervised clustering on hourly price-derived features can serve as an effective pair discovery mechanism for semiconductor equities. Three algorithms---OPTICS, DBSCAN, and KMeans---independently converge on the same set of structurally related pairs, which are validated through classical pairs trading criteria and out-of-sample backtesting.

The framework reliably identifies genuine relationships (26/33 pairs pass at least two of three classical criteria), and approximately half of tradeable pairs produce positive out-of-sample returns under a simple z-score strategy. The top performers (NXPI--ON, CDNS--SNPS, QCOM--QRVO) show Sharpe ratios of 1.4--3.6 on unseen data.

Equally important are the negative findings: cross-algorithm consensus does not predict out-of-sample profitability, and the gap between in-sample and out-of-sample results highlights the essential role of proper validation methodology.
